The Junior Developer With 7 Years Experience

I met a junior developer with 7 years experience. His resume actually looked pretty good. When we started the interview though… oof.

He had been writing JS for nearly a decade but could not use ES6.

He struggled with a simple problem that required iterating over an array and merging some objects.

How was this possible?

My guess is that he had spent all the time at his current company basically doing the same tasks over and over and over. I’m sure he was excellent at THAT particular company.

We could not move forward with him.

What I wish I had done: Pull him to the side after the interview and explain what he needs to learn in order to do better at his next interview.

What I did: “Thanks for your time today.”

I think about this story a lot. You don’t want to fall into the same trap. You want you to get to Senior Developer at some point.

Let’s clear up some of the dumb advice you’ve read on LinkedIn:

  • Titles don’t matter
  • Seniors need at least [x] years of experience
  • Seniors are the best coders on the team
  • Get experience and the titles will come

They’re all right. And they’re all terribly, terribly wrong.

The Senior Developer title can be wildly different at each company. You still need to chase it.

There’s a bit of an unhealthy obsession developers (me included) have with getting to Senior but there is a good reason.

On one end of the developer spectrum is junior.

They are expendable for the most part. Cheap and plentiful and need lots of guidance. They are an important investment and also a bit of a gamble.

On the other end of the spectrum is the senior.

They can make or break your team through their influence on the codebase, team and processes. They squish the really tough bugs. They make sure the team doesn’t over commit or refactor unnecessarily. They’ve made costly mistakes and understand how to avoid them.

Seniors are in demand. They are hard to find and the salary jump from junior to senior can be significant.

The majority of your career will likely be spent in this role.

I’ve met senior developers in their 50’s and 60’s. They were too smart. Screw managing developers, these people just wanted to code and they were generally amazing to work with.

Fail quicker. Chase experiences. Just f*cking ask.

Getting to senior developer is as much about writing “good” code as it is about gathering experiences and software-adjacent knowledge.

  • When to buy vs build a solution
  • How teams work within an organization (especially design, product, data science devops and engineering)
  • Recognizing micro vs macro trends
  • Managing expectations and deadlines

So what do you do?

First, stop thinking so damn much. Action cures anxiety.

Raise your hand when a critical incident happens.

Take on a feature outside your comfort zone.

Read a book on design patterns and software architecture.

Ask your manager what is the team’s biggest pain point and try to solve it. Even if you cannot — it will give you a better understanding of the kind of knowledge you need to gather in order to do so.

Look up previous epics and tasks that seniors have completed. How did they solve it? Could you? If not, what knowledge are you missing?

The easiest way to track your progress towards senior is to look at the scope of your work.

If it is widening — you are on the right track. You should be going from making sure your code works, to making sure the component works to ensuring quality in the project to researching solutions for the team to introducing patterns that others can use in their own work and managing deadlines which require you to coordinate with multiple developers.

Oh yeah…

Don’t forget to ask.

Pull your manager or CTO or team lead to the side. Whisper in their ear (or actually don’t — that’s super creepy)

What do I specifically need to do in order to get closer to senior?

And lastly…

Be patient-ish. Jog towards senior, don’t sprint. Some companies have arbitrary YOE requirements. I think these are misguided for the most part. I don’t think you can be senior in year 1. I also don’t think taking on small CSS changes for 8 years will make you a senior either.
