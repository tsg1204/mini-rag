How I Gained 30k Followers, Lost 50 lbs and Switched Careers after 30

TLDR; habits.

On Easter Sunday of 2012, I stumbled into our family’s tiny condo at 6AM. I was drunk, coming down off an 8-ball of blow and had been in a small car accident with a buddy of mine. This was not an abnormal night for me.

An hour later, I would have an intervention.

There were tears, cussing and lots of doubt.

I had been smoking since 14, drinking since 17 and experimenting with drugs since 13.

The next year I was working as a software engineer and running 3 miles a day. I’m still sober in case you’re wondering. I do smoke a 1 or 2 cigars a year (my daughter hates it).

In the last decade, I’ve gone from a pudgy, booze-guzzling drug abuser to making my personal trainer’s fitness page, managing a team of software engineers, owning a business and becoming a cringy influencer on LinkedIn (don’t roll your eyes too hard).

Things aren’t all roses and candy canes. I still have strong urges to back slide and I have serious self doubt. I honestly don’t know if I should be writing these kinds of articles.

Will people think I’m bragging?
What if a future customer or employer reads this?
Who am I to tell you anything?

First off, I’m not here to sell you anything (unless you want to change careers into software — then I absolutely have a program called Parsity which you should check out).

Ok — sales hat is off. I sincerely want to share what I’ve learned in the hopes it can help you.

Whether you want to lose weight, change careers or quit hard drugs, you already know what you need to do for the most part. Knowledge is not the blocker.

Action is what you need.

Unfortunately, you probably believe building habits is a game of will power.

Which is why you lose the game.

The path to success and failure are the same.

Your habits will make or break you.

You are not who you think you are.
You are what you do.

Habits create grooves in our brain. They make our life easier by reducing cognitive overload.

They sneak into our lives in response to the emotional roller coaster that is life.

Making decisions is taxing. Habits are the shortcut for decision-making.

The problem is that most of us have not been intentional when we create habits. They just kind of snuck up on us.

Now we’re “stuck” with them.

  • You pass by a box of donuts at work and eat more than you expected.
  • You sit in bed and scroll endlessly.
  • You have a glass or 3 of wine after dinner.

It doesn’t have to be this way.

I stumbled upon the recipe for creating habits when I quit drugs and alcohol. When people ask how I did it, I could not articulate what my process was.

Turns out, I was following a proven method without even knowing it.

You cannot destroy a habit

But you can replace it.

Remember, your habit is a response to something.

Identify what that something is.

For me, I liked to get wasted because I was insecure. It was also my only hobby. The first weekend I spent without drinking made me painfully aware of just how long a sober day is.

It’s long af.

When I got the urge to drink I would take a walk to the liquor store near our house and buy snacks and lotto tickets. I stuffed my face with candy and scratched hundreds of dollars of tickets for a few months to fight off the urges.

It worked.

I also became pretty fluffy and broke.

Then something interesting happened.

What’s your trigger?

I was sober but fat.

I didn’t like my job.

I still didn’t feel any more confident.

Then I found coding. My true replacement addiction. I am now owner of Parsity.io — an online coding school where we turn busy career changers into professional coders.

Learning how to code satisfied 2 of 3 requirements for replacing my old habit of getting obliterated:

  1. It gave me confidence. I felt smarter and proud to learn something difficult.
  2. I had something to do on my weekends and week nights.

There was still something missing.

At the heart of my addiction was deep insecurity. I still struggle with this. Coding made me feel better but I still didn’t like what I saw in the mirror.

I had no clue what to do in a gym so I decided to start running at the field across the street from our house.

I was over 200 pounds and my lungs were toast from 15 years of smoking menthols.

I could barely run half a mile.

Running released some of that sweet dopamine I craved and it only took 20 minutes to get it cranking.

The dopamine kept me coming back. Once my body started changing I was too afraid to stop.

Compound interest

I had identified my triggers — free time in the evenings and insecurity.

My new habits were responses to the same cues.

I got a rush from the running. I killed time with learning to code.

Then things snowballed.

At the risk of sounding like a cringy influencer, I did all the things cringy influencers do:

  • Meditating
  • Journaling
  • Reading self-help books
  • Saving and investing

Meme coin scam coming soon!

One habit led to another and yet another. Within a year my life changed dramatically.

In the next 5 years, I was unrecognizable.

I used the same pattern to quit drinking that I used to start journaling in the evenings when I would usually scroll on my phone.

I identified my trigger — going to bed.

To interrupt the pattern with a replacement, I simply bought a 99 cent journal and put it on my night stand.

Sounds too simple right?

Try it.

The dark side of habits

I never went to AA, therapy or had any professional treatment for my addictions.

My obsessive mindset might not be normal or even healthy.

I work nearly 7 days a week to keep myself busy. I don’t need to work this much but I do.

The day after I had a heart-related issue, I was back to doing 300 push ups at the park. My habit took over my decision making process. It was foolish in hindsight.

Creating habits is a super-power. With great power comes great responsibility.

What’s behind the habit you want to create?

How long should it last and when can it be “retired”?

At this point, I am more intentional with my habits and I strongly suggest you think about the purpose of your habits and the kind of person you want to be.

Here’s a question I have for you — “What do you truly want?”

Hard one huh?

I’m still trying to figure that one out myself. It’s a hell of a lot easier when you have habits that aren’t walking you down a path you never intended.

If this article helped in any way then I’m slightly happier.

Good luck you bandicoot.
