How to Negotiate Your Salary as a Software Developer

(and Stop Leaving Money on the Table)

Most developers are terrible negotiators.

I know, because for years I was one of them.

For my first 3 jobs, I had no clue about salary negotiations. No one ever taught me what I’m going to share with you.

Over the years, I’ve personally negotiated around $80–90K more in compensation using this exact method.

Here’s a snapshot of my salary trajectory:

  • 1st job — $60K. (OMG my first role! I should be grateful!)
  • 2nd job — $120K. (OMG — this is more money than anyone I know makes)
  • 3rd job — $120K. (Eh, ok.)
  • 3rd job (again) — $150K (I had a competing offer and they matched. Wait — so you had that extra money the whole time?)
  • 4th job — $165K (I asked for $180K and they went from $150K to $165K with a $20K sign on)
  • Current role — $200K (Negotiated from $180K to their highest band)

I did not include other, non-monetary things. Those amounts were my base salary at each role NOT including stocks/bonuses etc.

Today, I’m going to walk you through how to negotiate your salary as a software developer without sounding greedy, awkward, or ungrateful.

Because if you’ve ever been so thrilled to land an offer that you just blurt out “yes”… you’re not alone.

But it’s costing you tens of thousands of dollars.

Why Most Developers Fail to Negotiate

Unfortunately, most people never learn the rules of the game.

  • Scarcity mindset. You believe there aren’t enough jobs out there, so you’re lucky to even get one.
  • Fear of rejection. You think asking for more will make them pull the offer.
  • Over-researching. You waste hours on Glassdoor trying to find a “fair” number instead of just asking.
  • Needing a reason. You convince yourself you can’t ask without data or a justification.

All of this leads to the same outcome: you take whatever number they give you and hope for the best.

The problem? That’s not how the game works.

The Good News

Negotiation isn’t some dark art.

You can learn it and it works for juniors, seniors, bootcamp grads, everyone.

You don’t need multiple offers. You don’t need 10 years of experience.

You just need to ask, in the right way.

Step 1: Ditch the Research Rabbit Hole

Stop obsessing over salary websites. The company already knows the market rate.

If they’ve made you an offer, it’s rarely their ceiling.

Even if the number seems big compared to what you’ve made before, assume there’s still room. There usually is.

Step 2: Use This Simple Script

Here’s the exact script I use:

“I’m really grateful for the opportunity and excited about the role. I was hoping to come closer to this range [x] however. I’m happy to chat more and eager to join the team. Please let me know your thoughts.”

That’s it.

Don’t over-explain. Don’t justify. Just say it, pause, and wait.

If you want a number: 10–15% above what they offered is usually fair.

It signals confidence, not greed.

Step 3: Avoid the Rookie Mistake

The only way to blow an offer is to ask for something absurd (like double) or act entitled.

Otherwise, the worst you’ll hear is “we can’t do that.”

And even then, you’ve shown that you know your worth which often comes back around during the first raise cycle.

Step 4: Negotiate Beyond Salary

If they can’t move on cash, shift to other levers:

  • Sign-on bonus (companies will often use these to appease you. It’s one-time money and you get taxed ~40%)
  • Stocks or profit sharing
  • Remote or hybrid flexibility
  • Extra PTO or training budget

At one job, I negotiated to work from home 2.5 days a week and only be in the office from 10 a.m. — 2 p.m.

I had the same workload and expectations, just more freedom to get my kids from school and avoid a super long commute.

That was worth it to me.

Step 5: Remember Who Holds the Leverage

If you’ve gotten an offer, they’ve already decided: you’re their person.

They’ve spent weeks interviewing, screening, and debating.

Starting that process over is expensive.

And here’s what most developers forget you’re not taking money from them personally.

They’re spending the company’s budget, not their own paycheck.

So ask.

Step 6: Think Long-Term

If they truly can’t budge, say this:

“Would it be possible to revisit compensation after six months based on performance?”

That one line signals maturity, and often opens the door for future raises.

This Isn’t Theory — It’s Proven

I’ve helped multiple Parsity students with negotiations. Most earn an average of $10K more just by asking.

One dude, Eric, went from $80K to $93K. He hadn’t been employed for 6 months so this was a particularly big ask.

Honestly… most students never follow my advice. They are too afraid to ask.

Meanwhile, as a manager, I personally hired for the same job at the same time with a $15K gap between the people. One negotiated and the other didn’t.

Fair? Of course not.

Do you honestly think companies are scrambling to pay you more?

Bless your heart.

You and I are playing a game and it’s important to know the rules.

Your Template

Here’s the exact message you can send after getting an offer:

Hi [Manager’s Name],
I’m really grateful for the offer and genuinely excited about the opportunity to join [your evil company].
I was hoping to come in a little closer to [some dollar amount around 10–15% more], based on my experience and the scope of the role.
I’m very interested and looking forward to next steps.
Best,
Your Name

That’s it.

Simple, confident, human.

Final Thoughts

Whether you’re making $60K or $160K, the game is the same and it’s already being played.

If you choose not to play, fine. But understand what that means:

You’re leaving money on the table that could’ve been yours.

So use the script. Ask for what you want.

And if you need a pep talk before you hit “send,” I’ve got you.

Until then…

Know the rules. Use the template. And YOLO.
